Tutorial 4 Exercises

4001CMD - Mathematical Skills for Computing Professionals

Functions

Problem 1

For the recursive function $s(1)=1$, $s(2)=2$, and $s(n+1)=2s(n)-s(n-1)$, compute $s(1)$ through $s(6)$, conjecture a closed formula, and prove it by induction.

Problem 2

Given $A=\{a,b,c,d\}$ and $B=\{1,2,3,4,5\}$ with functions $p:A\to B$, $q:B\to B$, $r:B\to A$ as in the worksheet table, determine which are injective, surjective, and bijective.

Problem 3

Classify the functions $f(n)=n^3$, $g(n)=n+3$, and $h(n)=\begin{cases}n+1&n\text{ odd}\\n-1&n\text{ even}\end{cases}$ from $\mathbb N$ to $\mathbb N$ as injective/surjective/bijective.

Problem 4

For the Collatz-type recursive function $u(1)=1$ and $u(n+1)=u(n)/2$ if even, otherwise $3u(n)+1$: (a) show not injective/surjective, (b) repeat with $u(1)=5$, (c) test another starting value.

Problem 5

Let $s(x)=x+1$ and $t(x)=2x$. Show that $t\circ s$ and $s\circ t$ are different and give explicit formulas for each.

Problem 6

Prove that if $f$ and $g$ are bijections and $g\circ f$ is defined, then $(g\circ f)^{-1}=f^{-1}\circ g^{-1}$.

Problem 7

Show that $f:X\to Y$ has a left inverse iff $f$ is injective.

Problem 8

For $X=\{1,2,3,4,5\}$ and $f(1)=2,f(2)=2,f(3)=4,f(4)=4,f(5)=4$: (a) show $f\circ f=f$, (b) find a different $g$ such that $f\circ g=f$ and $g\circ f=f$.

Problem 9

Construct bijections $s,t:S\to S$ for $S=\{1,2,3\}$ with (a) $s\circ s=i$, $s\ne i$, and (b) $t\circ t\circ t=i$, $t\ne i$. Describe $s^{-1}$ and $t^{-1}$.

Problem 10

For $S=\{1,2,3\}$, how many bijections $f:S\to S$ are there, and how many satisfy $f=f^{-1}$?

Cardinality and Counting

Problem 11

Prove uniqueness of finite cardinality: if $f:A\to\{1,\ldots,m\}$ and $g:A\to\{1,\ldots,n\}$ are bijections, then $m=n$.

Problem 12

Let $X=\{1,2,3\}$ and $Y=\{0,1\}$. (a) Give one function $f:X\to Y$. (b) List all functions and show there are 8.

Problem 13

If $|X|=m$ and $|Y|=2$, find the size of the set of functions $X\to Y$. Conjecture and prove by induction.

Problem 14

Assign 4 students to 7 tutors, with no tutor taking more than one student. How many assignments are possible?

Problem 15

A drawer has 10 grey and 10 brown socks. What is the minimum number selected to guarantee a matching pair?

Problem 16

Show any set $S$ of 12 natural numbers contains two distinct $s_1,s_2$ such that $s_1-s_2$ is a multiple of 11.

Problem 17

For $X\subseteq\{1,\ldots,2n\}$ and odd set $Y=\{1,3,\ldots,2n-1\}$, define $f(x)$ as the greatest odd divisor in $Y$. Show if $|X|\ge n+1$, then $f$ is not injective and deduce two distinct elements with one a multiple of the other.

Problem 18

With notation from Problem 17, show there exists $X\subseteq\{1,\ldots,2n\}$ of size $n$ such that no member is a multiple of another.

Problem 19

Construct bijections from $\mathbb N$ to each set: (a) $\{n\in\mathbb N:n\ge 10^6\}$ and (b) the set of multiples of 3.

Problem 20

Prove: if $X\subseteq Y$ and $X$ is infinite, then $Y$ is infinite.

Problem 21

Let $X$ be a non-empty subset of $\mathbb{N}$ which has no greatest member.

  1. Using the Axioms of Algebra and the results of Week 2's lectures (such as the well-ordering principle), show that we can choose members $x_1, x_2, \ldots$ of $X$ such that $x_{n+1} > x_n$ for all $n \in \mathbb{N}$.
  2. Use the preceding exercise to conclude that $X$ is infinite.
  3. Prove the converse: that if $X$ is infinite, there is a sequence $x_1, x_2, \ldots$ with $x_{n+1} > x_n$ for all $n$.
