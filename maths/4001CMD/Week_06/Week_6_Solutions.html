Divisibility

Problem 1

Show that if $c|a$ and $c|b$, then $c|(xa+yb)$ for any integers $x,y \in \Z$.

View Solution

Solution & Explanation

Step 1: Write down the definitions of divisibility.

The statement $c|a$ ("c divides a") means that there exists some integer $k_1$ such that $a = c \cdot k_1$.

Similarly, $c|b$ means that there exists some integer $k_2$ such that $b = c \cdot k_2$.

Step 2: Substitute these definitions into the expression $xa+yb$.

We want to show that $xa+yb$ is also a multiple of $c$. Let's substitute the expressions for $a$ and $b$ from Step 1:

$$ xa + yb = x(ck_1) + y(ck_2) $$

Step 3: Factor out the common term $c$.

Using the distributive property, we can factor $c$ out of both terms on the right-hand side:

$$ xa + yb = c(xk_1 + yk_2) $$

Step 4: Conclude based on the definition of divisibility.

Let $k_3 = xk_1 + yk_2$. Since $x$, $y$, $k_1$, and $k_2$ are all integers, their products and sums must also be an integer. So, $k_3$ is an integer.

We can now write the expression as:

$$ xa + yb = c \cdot k_3 $$

This is precisely the definition of $c$ dividing $xa+yb$. Therefore, we have shown that $c|(xa+yb)$.

Problem 2

Find the quotient $q$ and remainder $r$ when $a$ is divided by $b$ in the following two cases.

  1. (a) $a=1001$, $b = 11$
  2. (b) $a = 12345$, $b = 234$
View Solution

Solution & Explanation

(a) $a=1001$, $b = 11$

We perform long division for $1001 \div 11$:

   91
  ---
11|1001
   -99
   ---
     11
    -11
    ---
      0

The division is exact. This means we can write $1001 = 11 \times 91 + 0$.

Quotient $q = 91$, Remainder $r = 0$.

(b) $a=12345$, $b = 234$

We perform long division for $12345 \div 234$:

   52
   ----
234|12345
   -1170  (234 * 5)
   -----
     645
    -468  (234 * 2)
    ----
     177

The division results in a quotient of 52 with a remainder of 177. This means we can write $12345 = 234 \times 52 + 177$.

Quotient $q = 52$, Remainder $r = 177$.

Problem 4

Find the decimal (base 10) representations of (a) $(11011101)_2$, and (b) $(4165)_7$.

View Solution

Solution & Explanation

To convert a number from base $b$ to base 10, we sum the products of each digit with $b$ raised to the power of the digit's position (starting from 0 on the right).

(a) $(11011101)_2$

The positions (from right to left) are 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7.

$$ (11011101)_2 = 1 \cdot 2^7 + 1 \cdot 2^6 + 0 \cdot 2^5 + 1 \cdot 2^4 + 1 \cdot 2^3 + 1 \cdot 2^2 + 0 \cdot 2^1 + 1 \cdot 2^0 $$ $$ = 1 \cdot 128 + 1 \cdot 64 + 0 \cdot 32 + 1 \cdot 16 + 1 \cdot 8 + 1 \cdot 4 + 0 \cdot 2 + 1 \cdot 1 $$ $$ = 128 + 64 + 0 + 16 + 8 + 4 + 0 + 1 $$ $$ = 221 $$

So, $(11011101)_2 = (221)_{10}$.

(b) $(4165)_7$

The positions (from right to left) are 0, 1, 2, 3.

$$ (4165)_7 = 4 \cdot 7^3 + 1 \cdot 7^2 + 6 \cdot 7^1 + 5 \cdot 7^0 $$ $$ = 4 \cdot 343 + 1 \cdot 49 + 6 \cdot 7 + 5 \cdot 1 $$ $$ = 1372 + 49 + 42 + 5 $$ $$ = 1468 $$

So, $(4165)_7 = (1468)_{10}$.

Problem 5

Find the $\gcd$ of 721 and 448 and express it in the form $721m + 448n$ for some $m,n \in \Z$.

View Solution

Solution & Explanation

Step 1: Use the Euclidean Algorithm to find the $\gcd$.

We repeatedly apply the division algorithm $a = bq + r$ until the remainder is 0. The $\gcd$ is the last non-zero remainder.

(1)  721 = 1 × 448 + 273
(2)  448 = 1 × 273 + 175
(3)  273 = 1 × 175 + 98
(4)  175 = 1 ×  98 + 77
(5)   98 = 1 ×  77 + 21
(6)   77 = 3 ×  21 + 14
(7)   21 = 1 ×  14 + 7
(8)   14 = 2 ×   7 + 0

The last non-zero remainder is 7. Therefore, $\gcd(721, 448) = 7$.

Step 2: Work backwards (Extended Euclidean Algorithm) to express 7 as a linear combination.

We start from equation (7) and substitute remainders from the equations above it.

7 = 21 - 1 × 14                 (from 7)
  = 21 - 1 × (77 - 3 × 21)     (sub 14 from 6)
  = 1 × 21 - 1 × 77 + 3 × 21
  = 4 × 21 - 1 × 77
  = 4 × (98 - 1 × 77) - 1 × 77     (sub 21 from 5)
  = 4 × 98 - 4 × 77 - 1 × 77
  = 4 × 98 - 5 × 77
  = 4 × 98 - 5 × (175 - 1 × 98)     (sub 77 from 4)
  = 4 × 98 - 5 × 175 + 5 × 98
  = 9 × 98 - 5 × 175
  = 9 × (273 - 1 × 175) - 5 × 175 (sub 98 from 3)
  = 9 × 273 - 9 × 175 - 5 × 175
  = 9 × 273 - 14 × 175
  = 9 × 273 - 14 × (448 - 1 × 273) (sub 175 from 2)
  = 9 × 273 - 14 × 448 + 14 × 273
  = 23 × 273 - 14 × 448
  = 23 × (721 - 1 × 448) - 14 × 448 (sub 273 from 1)
  = 23 × 721 - 23 × 448 - 14 × 448
  = 23 × 721 - 37 × 448

Step 3: Identify $m$ and $n$.

We have successfully expressed 7 in the form $721m + 448n$:

$$ 7 = 721 \cdot (23) + 448 \cdot (-37) $$

Thus, $m = 23$ and $n = -37$.

Problem 6

Find integers $x$ and $y$ satisfying $966x + 686y = 70$.

View Solution

Solution & Explanation

This is a linear Diophantine equation. We first find $\gcd(966, 686)$ using the Euclidean Algorithm. Solutions exist if and only if the $\gcd$ divides 70.

Step 1: Find $\gcd(966, 686)$.

(1)  966 = 1 × 686 + 280
(2)  686 = 2 × 280 + 126
(3)  280 = 2 × 126 + 28
(4)  126 = 4 ×  28 + 14
(5)   28 = 2 ×  14 + 0

The $\gcd(966, 686) = 14$. Since $14$ divides $70$ (specifically, $70 = 5 \times 14$), solutions exist.

Step 2: Work backwards to solve $966m + 686n = 14$.

14 = 126 - 4 × 28                 (from 4)
   = 126 - 4 × (280 - 2 × 126)     (sub 28 from 3)
   = 1 × 126 - 4 × 280 + 8 × 126
   = 9 × 126 - 4 × 280
   = 9 × (686 - 2 × 280) - 4 × 280 (sub 126 from 2)
   = 9 × 686 - 18 × 280 - 4 × 280
   = 9 × 686 - 22 × 280
   = 9 × 686 - 22 × (966 - 1 × 686) (sub 280 from 1)
   = 9 × 686 - 22 × 966 + 22 × 686
   = 31 × 686 - 22 × 966

So, we have $966(-22) + 686(31) = 14$. This gives us a solution $(m, n) = (-22, 31)$ for the equation equal to 14.

Step 3: Scale the solution to match the target value of 70.

We want the right-hand side to be $70$. We multiply our entire equation by $70 / 14 = 5$.

$$ 5 \times ( 966(-22) + 686(31) ) = 5 \times 14 $$ $$ 966(-22 \times 5) + 686(31 \times 5) = 70 $$ $$ 966(-110) + 686(155) = 70 $$

One pair of integers satisfying the equation is $x = -110$ and $y = 155$.

Problem 9

Show that if $n \geq 2$ and $n$ is not a prime, then there is a prime $p$ such that $p | n$ and $p^2 \leq n$. Hence show that if $467$ were not a prime, then it would have a prime divisor $p \leq 19$. Deduce that 467 is prime.

View Solution

Solution & Explanation

Part 1: Show $p|n$ and $p^2 \le n$.

If $n \ge 2$ is not prime, it is composite. By definition, this means $n$ can be written as a product of two integers $n = a \cdot b$, where $1 < a \le b < n$.

We must have $a \le \sqrt{n}$. Why? If $a > \sqrt{n}$, then $b$ (which is $\ge a$) would also be $> \sqrt{n}$. This would mean $a \cdot b > \sqrt{n} \cdot \sqrt{n} = n$. This contradicts $n = ab$. Therefore, the smallest factor $a$ must be less than or equal to $\sqrt{n}$.

Since $a > 1$, by the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic, $a$ must have at least one prime divisor, let's call it $p$.

Since $p$ divides $a$, we have $p \le a$.

Since $p|a$ and $a|n$, it follows that $p|n$.

Combining our inequalities: $p \le a \le \sqrt{n}$. This gives us $p \le \sqrt{n}$, and squaring both sides gives $p^2 \le n$.

Part 2: Show $p \le 19$ for $n=467$.

If 467 were not prime, by Part 1, it must have a prime divisor $p$ such that $p^2 \le 467$.

Taking the square root: $p \le \sqrt{467}$.

$\sqrt{467} \approx 21.61$. The primes less than or equal to 21.61 are: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19. The largest of these is 19. Thus, any prime factor $p$ must be $p \le 19$.

Part 3: Deduce 467 is prime.

We only need to check for divisibility by $p \in \{2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19\}$.

  • 2: No (467 is odd).
  • 3: No (sum of digits $4+6+7=17$, not div by 3).
  • 5: No (doesn't end in 0 or 5).
  • 7: $467 \div 7 = 66$ r 5. No.
  • 11: $467 \div 11 = 42$ r 5. No.
  • 13: $467 \div 13 = 35$ r 12. No.
  • 17: $467 \div 17 = 27$ r 8. No.
  • 19: $467 \div 19 = 24$ r 11. No.

Since 467 is not divisible by any prime $p \le \sqrt{467}$, it cannot be composite. Therefore, 467 is prime.

Problem 11

Write down 9 rational numbers lying strictly between 17/100 and 4/25.

View Solution

Solution & Explanation

Step 1: Find a common denominator for the two fractions.

The first fraction is $17/100$.

The second fraction is $4/25$. To get a denominator of 100, we multiply the numerator and denominator by 4: $4/25 = (4 \times 4) / (25 \times 4) = 16/100$.

The problem is asking for 9 rational numbers strictly between $16/100$ and $17/100$. (Note: $17/100$ is the larger number).

Step 2: Find an equivalent "gap" with more integers.

Right now, there are no integers between 16 and 17. To find numbers in between, we can multiply the numerator and denominator of both fractions by a number, for example, 10.

Start: $16/100 = 160/1000$

End: $17/100 = 170/1000$

Step 3: List 9 numbers in this new gap.

We need numbers strictly between $160/1000$ and $170/1000$. We can simply pick the 9 integers between 160 and 170 for our numerators:

$161/1000, 162/1000, 163/1000, 164/1000, 165/1000, 166/1000, 167/1000, 168/1000, 169/1000$

(In decimal form, these are $0.161, 0.162, \dots, 0.169$, which are all between $0.16$ and $0.17$).

Problem 12

Express the following repeating decimals as fractions:

  1. (a) $0.32\overline{1}$
  2. (b) $0.56\overline{78}$
View Solution

Solution & Explanation

(a) $0.32\overline{1}$

Let $x = 0.32\overline{1} = 0.321111...$

Multiply $x$ by 100 to move the non-repeating part to the left of the decimal: $100x = 32.\overline{1} = 32.1111...$

Multiply $x$ by 1000 to move one repeating block to the left: $1000x = 321.\overline{1} = 321.1111...$

Now, subtract the two new equations to cancel the repeating tail:

 1000x = 321.111...
 - 100x =  32.111...
 -----------------
  900x = 289

Solving for $x$: $x = 289/900$.

(b) $0.56\overline{78}$

Let $x = 0.56\overline{78} = 0.56787878...$

Multiply $x$ by 100 to move the non-repeating part left: $100x = 56.\overline{78} = 56.787878...$

The repeating block has 2 digits. Multiply $100x$ by $10^2 = 100$ (or $x$ by $10000$) to move one repeating block left: $10000x = 5678.\overline{78} = 5678.787878...$

Subtract the two new equations:

 10000x = 5678.7878...
 -  100x =   56.7878...
 ---------------------
  9900x = 5622

Solving for $x$: $x = 5622/9900$. We can simplify this fraction. Both are divisible by 2: $2811/4950$. Sum of digits of 2811 is 12 (div by 3). Sum of digits of 4950 is 18 (div by 3). $2811 \div 3 = 937$. $4950 \div 3 = 1650$.

$x = 937/1650$.

Problem 14

Show that if there are integers $m,n$ such that $mu + nv = 1$, then $\gcd(u,v) = 1$. (Hint: Use contradiction.)

View Solution

Solution & Explanation

We are given that $mu + nv = 1$ for some integers $m, n, u, v$.

Let $d = \gcd(u,v)$. We want to show $d=1$.

Step 1: Assume for contradiction that $d > 1$.

By the definition of the greatest common divisor, $d$ must divide both $u$ and $v$.

Step 2: Use the result from Problem 1.

In Problem 1, we showed that if a number $d$ divides $u$ and $v$, then it must divide any linear combination $mu + nv$.

Therefore, $d | (mu + nv)$.

Step 3: Reach the contradiction.

From the problem statement, we know that $mu + nv = 1$.

Substituting this into our finding from Step 2, we get $d | 1$.

The only positive integer that divides 1 is 1 itself. This means $d$ must be 1.

This contradicts our assumption that $d > 1$.

Step 4: Conclude.

Our assumption led to a contradiction, so the assumption must be false. Therefore, $d$ cannot be greater than 1.

Since $d$ must be a positive integer, we conclude that $d = \gcd(u,v) = 1$.

Problem 15

Let $a$ and $b$ be positive integers and let $d = \gcd(a,b)$. Prove that there are integers $x$ and $y$ which satisfy the equation $ax + by = c$ if and only if $d|c$.

View Solution

Solution & Explanation

This is an "if and only if" proof, so we must prove two separate directions.

Direction 1: (Forward) $\implies$

Assume that integers $x$ and $y$ exist such that $ax + by = c$. We must show that $d|c$.

  1. Let $d = \gcd(a,b)$. By definition, $d|a$ and $d|b$.
  2. From Problem 1, we know that if $d$ divides $a$ and $b$, it must divide any linear combination $ax+by$.
  3. Therefore, $d | (ax + by)$.
  4. Since we assumed $ax + by = c$, we can substitute $c$ to get $d|c$.

This completes the first direction.

Direction 2: (Backward) $\impliedby$

Assume that $d|c$. We must show that integers $x$ and $y$ exist such that $ax + by = c$.

  1. By Bézout's Identity (which is what the Extended Euclidean Algorithm finds), we know that there exist integers $m$ and $n$ such that $am + bn = d$.
  2. We are given that $d|c$. By definition, this means there is some integer $k$ such that $c = d \cdot k$.
  3. Take the equation from step 1 and multiply the entire thing by $k$:
    4. $$ k(am + bn) = k(d) $$
  4. Distribute $k$ on the left and substitute $c$ on the right:
    5. $$ a(km) + b(kn) = c $$
  5. Now, we can choose our integers $x$ and $y$. Let $x = km$ and $y = kn$. Since $k$, $m$, and $n$ are all integers, $x$ and $y$ are also integers.
  6. We have found integers $x = km$ and $y = kn$ that satisfy $ax + by = c$.

This completes the second direction.

Since we have proven both directions, the statement is true.

Problem 16 (Challenge)

Let $H$ be the set $\{4n + 1 : n \in \N \cup \{0\}\} = \{1, 5, 9, 13, \ldots\}$.

  1. (a) Show that $H$ is \emph{closed under multiplication}.
  2. (b) An $H$-\emph{prime} is a member of $H$ (other than 1) not divisible by any member of $H$ other than 1 and itself. Find three distinct $H$-primes $a,b,c \in H$ such that $441 = ab = c^2$.
  3. (c) Is it true that every element of $H$ has a factorization into $H$-primes? Is it true that it is unique?
  4. (d) Explain why the proof for unique prime factorization in $\Z$ does not work here.
View Solution

Solution & Explanation

(a) Closure under multiplication

Let $h_1$ and $h_2$ be two members of $H$. Then $h_1 = 4n + 1$ and $h_2 = 4m + 1$ for some non-negative integers $n, m$.

Their product is:

$$ h_1 h_2 = (4n + 1)(4m + 1) = 16nm + 4n + 4m + 1 $$

We can factor out a 4:

$$ h_1 h_2 = 4(4nm + n + m) + 1 $$

Let $k = 4nm + n + m$. Since $n$ and $m$ are integers, $k$ is also an integer. The product is of the form $4k+1$, which is the definition of a member of $H$. Thus, $H$ is closed under multiplication.

(b) H-primes and 441

First, $441 = 4(110) + 1$, so $441 \in H$.

Let's find the regular prime factorization of 441 in $\Z$: $441 = 21^2 = (3 \times 7)^2 = 3^2 \times 7^2 = 9 \times 49$.

Now let's check the H-primality of the factors $9, 49, 21$:

  • Is $9 \in H$? $9 = 4(2) + 1$. Yes. Is it H-prime? Smallest H-number (not 1) is 5. $5 \times 5 = 25 > 9$. So 9 cannot be factored by H-numbers. 9 is H-prime.
  • Is $49 \in H$? $49 = 4(12) + 1$. Yes. Is it H-prime? $5 \times 5 = 25 \in H$. $5 \times 9 = 45 \in H$. $9 \times 9 = 81 \in H$. No product of H-numbers (other than 1 × 49) makes 49. 49 is H-prime.
  • Is $21 \in H$? $21 = 4(5) + 1$. Yes. Is it H-prime? $5 \times 5 = 25 > 21$. 21 is H-prime.

We need $a, b, c$ such that $441 = ab = c^2$.

From our factorization: $441 = 9 \times 49$ and $441 = 21^2$.

We can choose $a=9$, $b=49$, and $c=21$. All are distinct H-primes.

(c) Factorization and Uniqueness

Yes, it is true that every element has a factorization into H-primes (by a similar argument to standard prime factorization; you can always break it down until you can't anymore).

No, it is not true that the factorization is unique. As we just showed, $441$ has two different factorizations into H-primes: $441 = 9 \times 49$ and $441 = 21 \times 21$.

(d) Why the proof of uniqueness fails

The standard proof of unique prime factorization in $\Z$ relies on a critical property called Euclid's Lemma, which states: "If a prime $p$ divides a product $ab$, then $p$ must divide $a$ or $p$ must divide $b$."

Let's test this lemma for our H-primes.

Let the H-prime be $p_H = 21$. Let the product be $ab = 9 \times 49 = 441$.

Our H-prime $21$ certainly divides $441$ (since $441 = 21 \times 21$).

But does $21$ divide $9$? No (in $\Z$).

And does $21$ divide $49$? No (in $\Z$).

Euclid's Lemma fails in the set $H$. The H-prime $21$ divides the product $9 \times 49$, but it does not divide either $9$ or $49$. Because this key lemma fails, the entire proof of unique factorization collapses.

Problem 17

For which natural numbers $k$ in the range $1 \leq k \leq 100$ is it true that $\sqrt k$ is a rational number? Based on this answer, make a conjecture: does there exist a natural number $k \in \N$ for which $\sqrt k$ is a non-integer rational number?

View Solution

Solution & Explanation

A key theorem in number theory states that for any integer $k$, $\sqrt{k}$ is either an integer or it is irrational. It can never be a non-integer fraction.

Therefore, $\sqrt{k}$ is rational if and only if $\sqrt{k}$ is an integer.

$\sqrt{k}$ is an integer if and only if $k$ is a perfect square.

We need to find all perfect squares $k$ in the range $1 \leq k \leq 100$.

  • $1^2 = 1$
  • $2^2 = 4$
  • $3^2 = 9$
  • $4^2 = 16$
  • $5^2 = 25$
  • $6^2 = 36$
  • $7^2 = 49$
  • $8^2 = 64$
  • $9^2 = 81$
  • $10^2 = 100$

The set of such numbers is $\{1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100\}$.

Conjecture:

Based on this, we conjecture: No, there does not exist a natural number $k$ for which $\sqrt{k}$ is a non-integer rational number.

Problem 18

Suppose that $m^2 = kn^2$, where $k,m,n$ are all natural numbers. If the prime factorization of $m$ is $p_1^{e_1} p_2^{e_2} \cdots p_r^{e_r}$, what does the unique factorization tell us about the prime factorizations of $k$ and $n$? Deduce that the conjecture suggested by Problem 17 is correct.

View Solution

Solution & Explanation

We are given $m^2 = kn^2$. Let's rearrange this to $k = m^2 / n^2 = (m/n)^2$. The problem is equivalent to proving that if $\sqrt{k} = m/n$ (a rational number), then $k$ must be a perfect square (which implies $\sqrt{k}$ is an integer).

Step 1: Analyze the prime factorization of $m^2$ and $n^2$.

Let the prime factorization of $m$ be $m = p_1^{e_1} p_2^{e_2} \cdots p_r^{e_r}$.

Then the prime factorization of $m^2$ is $m^2 = (p_1^{e_1} \cdots p_r^{e_r})^2 = p_1^{2e_1} p_2^{2e_2} \cdots p_r^{2e_r}$.

Notice that all exponents in the prime factorization of $m^2$ are even numbers.

Similarly, let $n = p_1^{f_1} p_2^{f_2} \cdots p_r^{f_r}$ (using the same set of primes, where $f_i$ can be 0). Then $n^2 = p_1^{2f_1} p_2^{2f_2} \cdots p_r^{2f_r}$. All exponents are also even.

Step 2: Analyze the prime factorization of $k$.

From $m^2 = kn^2$, we have $k = m^2 / n^2$.

Using the factorizations from Step 1:

$$ k = \frac{p_1^{2e_1} p_2^{2e_2} \cdots p_r^{e_r}}{p_1^{2f_1} p_2^{2f_2} \cdots p_r^{2f_r}} $$ $$ k = p_1^{2e_1 - 2f_1} p_2^{2e_2 - 2f_2} \cdots p_r^{2e_r - 2f_r} $$ $$ k = p_1^{2(e_1 - f_1)} p_2^{2(e_2 - f_2)} \cdots p_r^{2(e_r - f_r)} $$

Step 3: Draw a conclusion about $k$.

By the uniqueness of prime factorizations, this must be the prime factorization of $k$.

Look at every exponent in the factorization of $k$: $2(e_1 - f_1)$, $2(e_2 - f_2)$, etc. They are all, by definition, even numbers.

A number whose prime factorization contains only even exponents is a perfect square. We can see this by letting $j = p_1^{e_1 - f_1} p_2^{e_2 - f_2} \cdots p_r^{e_r - f_r}$. Then $k = j^2$.

Step 4: Deduce the conjecture is correct.

We started by assuming $\sqrt{k}$ is rational, i.e., $\sqrt{k} = m/n$. This led to $m^2 = kn^2$.

Our analysis showed that this equality implies $k$ must be a perfect square (e.g., $k = j^2$ for some integer $j$).

If $k$ is a perfect square, then $\sqrt{k} = \sqrt{j^2} = j$, which is an integer.

Therefore, if $\sqrt{k}$ is rational, it must be an integer. It cannot be a non-integer rational number. The conjecture from Problem 17 is correct.

Problem 19

When $m = 7$ and $n=5$, we have $m^2 - 2n^2 = -1$. Show that if $p_{i+1}$ and $q_{i+1}$ are defined in terms of $p_i$ and $q_i$ by the equations

$$ p_{i+1} = p_i^2 + 2q_i^2 \quad \textrm{and} \quad q_{i+1} = 2p_i q_i $$

then $p_{i+1}^2 - 2q_{i+1}^2 = \left(p_i^2 - 2q_i^2\right)^2$. Hence show that there are infinitely many distinct pairs of natural numbers $x,y$ such that $x^2 - 2y^2 = 1$.

View Solution

Solution & Explanation

Part 1: Prove the identity.

We start with the left-hand side (LHS), $p_{i+1}^2 - 2q_{i+1}^2$, and substitute the definitions of $p_{i+1}$ and $q_{i+1}$.

$$ \text{LHS} = (p_i^2 + 2q_i^2)^2 - 2(2p_i q_i)^2 $$

Expand the terms:

$$ \text{LHS} = (p_i^4 + 4p_i^2 q_i^2 + 4q_i^4) - 2(4p_i^2 q_i^2) $$ $$ \text{LHS} = p_i^4 + 4p_i^2 q_i^2 + 4q_i^4 - 8p_i^2 q_i^2 $$

Combine the middle terms:

$$ \text{LHS} = p_i^4 - 4p_i^2 q_i^2 + 4q_i^4 $$

Now let's expand the right-hand side (RHS), $(p_i^2 - 2q_i^2)^2$:

$$ \text{RHS} = (p_i^2)^2 - 2(p_i^2)(2q_i^2) + (2q_i^2)^2 $$ $$ \text{RHS} = p_i^4 - 4p_i^2 q_i^2 + 4q_i^4 $$

Since LHS = RHS, the identity $p_{i+1}^2 - 2q_{i+1}^2 = (p_i^2 - 2q_i^2)^2$ is proven.

Part 2: Show infinitely many solutions to $x^2 - 2y^2 = 1$.

We are given a starting pair $(p_1, q_1) = (7, 5)$ for which $p_1^2 - 2q_1^2 = 7^2 - 2(5^2) = 49 - 50 = -1$.

Let's use the recurrence to find the next pair, $(p_2, q_2)$:

$p_2 = p_1^2 + 2q_1^2 = 7^2 + 2(5^2) = 49 + 50 = 99$.

$q_2 = 2p_1 q_1 = 2(7)(5) = 70$.

Now let's check the value of $p_2^2 - 2q_2^2$ using our identity:

$$ p_2^2 - 2q_2^2 = (p_1^2 - 2q_1^2)^2 = (-1)^2 = 1 $$

So, $(x, y) = (99, 70)$ is a solution to $x^2 - 2y^2 = 1$.

Now, let's find the next pair, $(p_3, q_3)$, from $(p_2, q_2) = (99, 70)$.

$p_3 = 99^2 + 2(70^2) = 9801 + 2(4900) = 9801 + 9800 = 19601$.

$q_3 = 2(99)(70) = 13860$.

Let's check $p_3^2 - 2q_3^2$ using the identity:

$$ p_3^2 - 2q_3^2 = (p_2^2 - 2q_2^2)^2 = (1)^2 = 1 $$

So, $(x, y) = (19601, 13860)$ is another solution.

Since $p_{i+1}$ and $q_{i+1}$ are defined by sums and products of positive numbers, the sequences $(p_i)$ and $(q_i)$ are strictly increasing for $i \ge 2$.

This process can be repeated infinitely, generating a new, larger, distinct pair $(p_{i+1}, q_{i+1})$ from any pair $(p_i, q_i)$ that satisfies $p_i^2 - 2q_i^2 = 1$. Thus, there are infinitely many distinct pairs.

Problem 20

  1. (a) Let $k \in \N$. Show that the numbers defined recursively by $$ x_1 = k, \quad x_{n+1} = \frac 1 2 \left(x_n + \frac k{x_n}\right) \quad (n \geq 1) $$ approach $\sqrt k$. (You may assume that there exists some $X \in \R$ so that $x_n$ approaches $X$.)
  2. (b) Use part (a) to approximate $\sqrt 3$ and $\sqrt 5$ to six decimal places.

Solution & Explanation

(a) Show the limit is $\sqrt{k}$.

We are given the recurrence $x_{n+1} = \frac 1 2 \left(x_n + \frac k{x_n}\right)$ and we assume that $x_n$ approaches a limit $X$.

This means that as $n \to \infty$, both $x_n \to X$ and $x_{n+1} \to X$. We can substitute $X$ for both $x_n$ and $x_{n+1}$ in the equation to find the value of the limit:

$$ X = \frac 1 2 \left(X + \frac k{X}\right) $$

Now, we solve for $X$:

$$ 2X = X + \frac k{X} $$ $$ 2X - X = \frac k{X} $$ $$ X = \frac k{X} $$ $$ X^2 = k $$ $$ X = \pm \sqrt{k} $$

Since $x_1 = k$ is a positive number, and the recurrence $x_{n+1}$ is an average of two positive terms ($x_n$ and $k/x_n$), all terms $x_n$ will be positive. Therefore, their limit $X$ must be non-negative. This rules out the negative root.

Thus, the limit $X$ must be $\sqrt{k}$.

(b) Approximate $\sqrt{3}$ and $\sqrt{5}$.

For $\sqrt{3}$: Let $k=3$ and $x_1 = 3$ (or $x_1=1.7$). Let's start with $x_1 = 2$.

$x_1 = 2$

$x_2 = 0.5(x_1 + 3/x_1) = 0.5(2 + 3/2) = 0.5(3.5) = 1.75$

$x_3 = 0.5(x_2 + 3/x_2) = 0.5(1.75 + 3/1.75) \approx 0.5(1.75 + 1.71428571) \approx 1.73214286$

$x_4 = 0.5(x_3 + 3/x_3) = 0.5(1.73214286 + 3/1.73214286) \approx 0.5(1.73214286 + 1.73195880) \approx 1.73205083$

$x_5 = 0.5(x_4 + 3/x_4) = 0.5(1.73205083 + 3/1.73205083) \approx 0.5(1.73205083 + 1.73205078) \approx 1.73205081$

The value is stable to many places. $\sqrt{3} \approx$ 1.732051.

For $\sqrt{5}$: Let $k=5$ and $x_1 = 2$.

$x_1 = 2$

$x_2 = 0.5(x_1 + 5/x_1) = 0.5(2 + 5/2) = 0.5(4.5) = 2.25$

$x_3 = 0.5(x_2 + 5/x_2) = 0.5(2.25 + 5/2.25) \approx 0.5(2.25 + 2.22222222) \approx 2.23611111$

The value is stable. $\sqrt{5} \approx$ 2.236068.

Problem 21

In class, we showed that the sequence $(x_n)$ recursively defined by $$ x_1 = 2, \quad x_{n+1} = \frac 12 \left(x_n + \frac 2{x_n}\right) $$ approaches $\sqrt 2$. These terms $x_n$ are all rational numbers: $x_n = p_n/q_n$. Show that $$ p_n^2 - 2q_n^2 = 1 \quad \textrm{for all } n \geq 2. $$

View Solution

Solution & Explanation

This proof is best done by induction.

Step 1: Find the recurrence for $p_n$ and $q_n$.

We have $x_n = p_n/q_n$. Let's substitute this into the recurrence for $x_{n+1}$.

$$ x_{n+1} = \frac{p_{n+1}}{q_{n+1}} = \frac 1 2 \left(\frac{p_n}{q_n} + \frac{2}{p_n/q_n}\right) $$ $$ \frac{p_{n+1}}{q_{n+1}} = \frac 1 2 \left(\frac{p_n}{q_n} + \frac{2q_n}{p_n}\right) $$

Find a common denominator on the right side:

$$ \frac{p_{n+1}}{q_{n+1}} = \frac 1 2 \left(\frac{p_n^2 + 2q_n^2}{p_n q_n}\right) $$ $$ \frac{p_{n+1}}{q_{n+1}} = \frac{p_n^2 + 2q_n^2}{2p_n q_n} $$

This gives us a recurrence relation for the numerator and denominator: $p_{n+1} = p_n^2 + 2q_n^2$ and $q_{n+1} = 2p_n q_n$. (Note: this is the exact same recurrence as Problem 19!)

Step 2: Establish the Base Case ($n=2$).

$n=1$: $x_1 = 2 = 2/1$. So, $p_1 = 2$ and $q_1 = 1$.

$p_1^2 - 2q_1^2 = 2^2 - 2(1^2) = 4 - 2 = 2$. (This is not 1, which is fine, the property starts at $n=2$).

$n=2$: We find $x_2$ from $x_1$.

$$ x_2 = \frac 1 2 \left(x_1 + \frac 2{x_1}\right) = \frac 1 2 \left(2 + \frac 2 2\right) = \frac 1 2 (2 + 1) = \frac 3 2 $$

So, $p_2 = 3$ and $q_2 = 2$.

Let's check the property for $n=2$: $p_2^2 - 2q_2^2 = 3^2 - 2(2^2) = 9 - 2(4) = 9 - 8 = 1$.

The base case holds. The property is true for $n=2$.

Step 3: Inductive Step.

Assume the property is true for $n=k$, where $k \ge 2$. That is, assume $p_k^2 - 2q_k^2 = 1$.

We must show it is true for $n=k+1$. We need to show that $p_{k+1}^2 - 2q_{k+1}^2 = 1$.

From Problem 19, we already proved the identity:

$$ p_{n+1}^2 - 2q_{n+1}^2 = (p_n^2 - 2q_n^2)^2 $$

Let $n=k$. This identity tells us:

$$ p_{k+1}^2 - 2q_{k+1}^2 = (p_k^2 - 2q_k^2)^2 $$

From our inductive assumption, we know $p_k^2 - 2q_k^2 = 1$.

Substitute this into the equation:

$$ p_{k+1}^2 - 2q_{k+1}^2 = (1)^2 = 1 $$

This is exactly what we needed to show. By induction, the statement $p_n^2 - 2q_n^2 = 1$ is true for all $n \geq 2$.
