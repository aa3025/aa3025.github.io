Mathematical Foundation: Why RSA Works

This proof demonstrates that applying the private key ($d$) to the ciphertext ($c$) recovers the original message ($m$).

Goal: Prove Decryption Recovers Message

We must show that the original message $m$ is congruent to the decrypted message $m'$ modulo $n$.

$$m^{ed} \equiv m \pmod{n}$$

1. The Key Algebraic Relationship

The proof begins with the defining property of the keys, which we established using the Extended Euclidean Algorithm: $d$ is the inverse of $e$ modulo the totient $\phi(n)$.

  • $n = p \cdot q$ (modulus)
  • $\phi(n) = z = (p-1)(q-1)$ (totient)
  • $ed \equiv 1 \pmod{z}$

This congruence means that $ed$ must equal 1 plus some multiple of $z$:

$$ed = 1 + k \cdot z \quad \text{for some integer } k \ge 1$$ $$ed = 1 + k(p-1)(q-1)$$

2. Divide and Conquer Strategy

Since $n = p \cdot q$, if we can prove the congruence holds for both $p$ and $q$ separately, it must hold for $n$:

  • If $m^{ed} \equiv m \pmod{p}$ AND
  • If $m^{ed} \equiv m \pmod{q}$ THEN
  • $m^{ed} \equiv m \pmod{n}$

We only need to prove the first congruence, as the second is identical.

3. Proof Modulo $p$ (Using Fermat's Little Theorem)

Case A: $p$ divides $m$ (i.e., $m$ is a multiple of $p$)

If $p$ divides $m$, then $m \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$.

$$m^{ed} \equiv 0^{ed} \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$$ $$\text{Since } m \equiv 0 \pmod{p}, \text{ the statement } m^{ed} \equiv m \pmod{p} \text{ holds.}$$

Case B: $p$ does NOT divide $m$

In this case, we rely on Fermat's Little Theorem (FLT), which states: $m^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$.

We rewrite the exponent $ed$ using the relationship from Section 1: $ed = 1 + k(p-1)(q-1)$.

$$m^{ed} = m^{1 + k(p-1)(q-1)}$$ $$m^{ed} = m^1 \cdot m^{k(p-1)(q-1)}$$ $$m^{ed} = m \cdot (m^{p-1})^{k(q-1)}$$

Now, we reduce the expression modulo $p$. Applying FLT, we replace $(m^{p-1})$ with $1$:

$$m^{ed} \equiv m \cdot (\mathbf{1})^{k(q-1)} \pmod{p}$$ $$m^{ed} \equiv m \cdot 1 \pmod{p}$$ $$m^{ed} \equiv m \pmod{p}$$

4. Final Conclusion

Since the congruence $m^{ed} \equiv m$ holds in both Case A and Case B (i.e., $m^{ed} \equiv m \pmod{p}$), and by symmetry it must also hold modulo $q$, we conclude:

$$\mathbf{m^{ed} \equiv m \pmod{n}}$$

This proves that the plaintext $m$ is successfully recovered when the ciphertext $c=m^e$ is raised to the private exponent $d$ modulo $n$.
