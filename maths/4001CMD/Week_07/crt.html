The Chinese Remainder Theorem

Core Idea in Simple Terms

Imagine this scenario:

  • You have a number. When you divide it by 3, the remainder is 2.
  • When you divide it by 5, the remainder is 3.
  • You are told the number is between 0 and 15.

What is the number?

You could try guessing: a number that fits is 8 (8 ÷ 3 = 2 remainder 2, and 8 ÷ 5 = 1 remainder 3). The Chinese Remainder Theorem is the formal, general rule that guarantees problems like this have a solution, and tells us how to find it.

In essence, the CRT says:

If you know the remainders of a number when divided by several pairwise coprime integers, you can uniquely determine that number modulo their product.

Formal Statement

Let \( n_1, n_2, ..., n_k \) be positive integers that are pairwise coprime (this means the greatest common divisor of any two of them is 1).

Let \( a_1, a_2, ..., a_k \) be any integers.

Then the system of simultaneous congruences:

\[ \begin{cases} x \equiv a_1 \pmod{n_1} \\ x \equiv a_2 \pmod{n_2} \\ \vdots \\ x \equiv a_k \pmod{n_k} \end{cases} \]

has a solution, and this solution is unique modulo \( N \), where \( N = n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdot ... \cdot n_k \).

In other words:

  1. Existence: There exists an integer \( x \) that satisfies all the congruences at once.
  2. Uniqueness: All solutions are congruent modulo \( N \), meaning if \( x \) and \( y \) are both solutions, then \( x \equiv y \pmod{N} \). So, there is essentially one solution between \( 0 \) and \( N-1 \).

How to Find the Solution (The Construction Method)

The theorem doesn't just say a solution exists; it gives us a recipe to find it. Here are the steps:

1. Compute the product: \( N = n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdot ... \cdot n_k \).

2. For each modulus \( n_i \), compute:

\( N_i = \frac{N}{n_i} \). (This is the product of all the other moduli)

3. Find the modular inverse: For each \( N_i \), find a number \( M_i \) such that \( N_i \cdot M_i \equiv 1 \pmod{n_i} \).

This \( M_i \) is the modular inverse of \( N_i \) modulo \( n_i \). We know it exists because \( \gcd(N_i, n_i) = 1 \) (since all \( n_i \) are coprime).

4. Combine the pieces: The solution \( x \) is given by:

\[ x = (a_1 \cdot N_1 \cdot M_1 + a_2 \cdot N_2 \cdot M_2 + ... + a_k \cdot N_k \cdot M_k) \mod N \]

Let's see this in action with our initial example.

Worked Example 1: Two Congruences

Find \( x \) such that:

\[ \begin{cases} x \equiv 2 \pmod{3} \\ x \equiv 3 \pmod{5} \end{cases} \]

1. Compute \( N \): \( N = 3 \cdot 5 = 15 \)

2. For each modulus:

  • For \( n_1 = 3 \): \( N_1 = N / n_1 = 15 / 3 = 5 \)
  • For \( n_2 = 5 \): \( N_2 = N / n_2 = 15 / 5 = 3 \)

3. Find the modular inverses:

  • Find \( M_1 \) such that \( 5 \cdot M_1 \equiv 1 \pmod{3} \).
  • \( 5 \equiv 2 \pmod{3} \), so we need \( 2 \cdot M_1 \equiv 1 \pmod{3} \).
  • Trying values: \( M_1 = 2 \) works because \( 2 \cdot 2 = 4 \equiv 1 \pmod{3} \).
  • Find \( M_2 \) such that \( 3 \cdot M_2 \equiv 1 \pmod{5} \).
  • \( M_2 = 2 \) works because \( 3 \cdot 2 = 6 \equiv 1 \pmod{5} \).

4. Combine the pieces:

\[ x = (a_1 N_1 M_1 + a_2 N_2 M_2) \mod N \] \[ x = (2 \cdot 5 \cdot 2 + 3 \cdot 3 \cdot 2) \mod 15 \] \[ x = (20 + 18) \mod 15 \] \[ x = 38 \mod 15 \] \[ x = 8 \]

Answer: The unique solution modulo 15 is \( x = 8 \). This matches our initial guess!

Worked Example 2: Three Congruences

Find the smallest positive integer \( x \) that satisfies:

\[ \begin{cases} x \equiv 2 \pmod{3} \\ x \equiv 3 \pmod{5} \\ x \equiv 2 \pmod{7} \end{cases} \]

1. Compute \( N \): \( N = 3 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 = 105 \)

2. For each modulus:

  • For \( n_1 = 3 \): \( N_1 = N / n_1 = 105 / 3 = 35 \)
  • For \( n_2 = 5 \): \( N_2 = N / n_2 = 105 / 5 = 21 \)
  • For \( n_3 = 7 \): \( N_3 = N / n_3 = 105 / 7 = 15 \)

3. Find the modular inverses:

  • Find \( M_1 \) such that \( 35 \cdot M_1 \equiv 1 \pmod{3} \).
  • \( 35 \equiv 2 \pmod{3} \), so we need \( 2 \cdot M_1 \equiv 1 \pmod{3} \).
  • \( M_1 = 2 \) works because \( 2 \cdot 2 = 4 \equiv 1 \pmod{3} \).
  • Find \( M_2 \) such that \( 21 \cdot M_2 \equiv 1 \pmod{5} \).
  • \( 21 \equiv 1 \pmod{5} \), so we need \( 1 \cdot M_2 \equiv 1 \pmod{5} \).
  • \( M_2 = 1 \) works because \( 21 \cdot 1 = 21 \equiv 1 \pmod{5} \).
  • Find \( M_3 \) such that \( 15 \cdot M_3 \equiv 1 \pmod{7} \).
  • \( 15 \equiv 1 \pmod{7} \), so we need \( 1 \cdot M_3 \equiv 1 \pmod{7} \).
  • \( M_3 = 1 \) works because \( 15 \cdot 1 = 15 \equiv 1 \pmod{7} \).

4. Combine the pieces:

\[ x = (a_1 N_1 M_1 + a_2 N_2 M_2 + a_3 N_3 M_3) \mod N \] \[ x = (2 \cdot 35 \cdot 2 + 3 \cdot 21 \cdot 1 + 2 \cdot 15 \cdot 1) \mod 105 \] \[ x = (140 + 63 + 30) \mod 105 \] \[ x = 233 \mod 105 \] \[ x = 233 - 2 \cdot 105 = 233 - 210 = 23 \]

Verification:

  • \( 23 \div 3 = 7 \) remainder \( 2 \) ✓
  • \( 23 \div 5 = 4 \) remainder \( 3 \) ✓
  • \( 23 \div 7 = 3 \) remainder \( 2 \) ✓

Answer: The smallest positive solution is \( x = 23 \). All other solutions are of the form \( x = 23 + 105k \) for integer \( k \).

Why is it so Important and Useful?

The CRT is not just a mathematical curiosity; it's a powerful tool.

1. Efficiency in Computations: Instead of working with one very large number (e.g., \( N = 1000 \)), you can break the problem down and work with several smaller, coprime numbers (e.g., \( 8, 125, \) and \( 1 \), since \( 8 \cdot 125 \cdot 1 = 1000 \)). This is much faster for computers. This is its primary use in RSA cryptography and computer algebra systems.

2. A Foundational Concept in Number Theory: The CRT proves that if you have pairwise coprime moduli \( n_1, n_2, ..., n_k \), then the ring of integers modulo \( N \) (denoted \( \mathbb{Z}/N\mathbb{Z} \)) is structurally identical to the product of the rings \( \mathbb{Z}/n_1\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/n_2\mathbb{Z} \times ... \times \mathbb{Z}/n_k\mathbb{Z} \). This is a profound result that connects different areas of mathematics.

A Crucial Caveat: The "Pairwise Coprime" Condition

The theorem requires the moduli \( n_1, n_2, ..., n_k \) to be pairwise coprime. If they are not, a solution might not exist.

Example of Failure:

Consider:

\[ \begin{cases} x \equiv 1 \pmod{2} \\ x \equiv 2 \pmod{4} \end{cases} \]

The moduli 2 and 4 are not coprime.

  • The first equation says \( x \) is odd.
  • The second equation says \( x \) is even (since \( x \equiv 2 \pmod{4} \) means \( x = 4k + 2 \), which is even).
  • A number cannot be both odd and even. Therefore, no solution exists.

Summary

The Chinese Remainder Theorem is a cornerstone of number theory that allows us to reconstruct an integer from its remainders modulo a set of coprime integers. It guarantees a unique solution and provides a clear, constructive method to find it, with far-reaching applications in computer science, cryptography, and abstract algebra.
