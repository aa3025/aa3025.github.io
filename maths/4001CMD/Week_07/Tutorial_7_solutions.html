Modular Arithmetic

Problem 1.1: Verification

(a) Verify $1,234,567 \times 90,123 \equiv 1 \pmod{10}$.

(b) Verify $2468 \times 13,579 \equiv -3 \pmod{25}$.

View Solution

Solution & Explanation

(a) Modulo 10

We only need the last digit of each number:

$$1,234,567 \equiv 7 \pmod{10} \quad \text{and} \quad 90,123 \equiv 3 \pmod{10}$$

Multiplying the remainders:

$$1,234,567 \times 90,123 \equiv 7 \times 3 \equiv 21 \equiv 1 \pmod{10}$$

(b) Modulo 25

We find the remainders modulo 25:

$$\begin{aligned} 2468 &= 2450 + 18 \equiv 18 \pmod{25} \\ 13,579 &= 13,575 + 4 \equiv 4 \pmod{25}\end{aligned}$$

Since $18 \equiv -7 \pmod{25}$ (because $18 - (-7) = 25$):

$$\begin{aligned} 2468 \times 13,579 &\equiv 18 \times 4 \pmod{25} \\ &\equiv 72 \pmod{25} \\ &\equiv 72 - 3(25) \equiv 72 - 75 \equiv -3 \pmod{25} \end{aligned}$$

Problem 1.2: Modular Inverses

Find the inverses of the following:

  1. (a) $2$ in $\mathbb{Z}_{11}$
  2. (b) $7$ in $\mathbb{Z}_{15}$
  3. (c) $7$ in $\mathbb{Z}_{16}$
  4. (d) $5$ in $\mathbb{Z}_{13}$
View Solution

Solution & Explanation

We are looking for a number $b$ such that $a \times b \equiv 1 \pmod{m}$.

(a) $2$ in $\mathbb{Z}_{11}$

$$2 \times 6 = 12 \equiv 1 \pmod{11} \implies 2^{-1} = 6$$

(b) $7$ in $\mathbb{Z}_{15}$

$$7 \times 13 = 91 \equiv 6 \times 15 + 1 \equiv 1 \pmod{15} \implies 7^{-1} = 13$$

(c) $7$ in $\mathbb{Z}_{16}$

$$7 \times 7 = 49 \equiv 3 \times 16 + 1 \equiv 1 \pmod{16} \implies 7^{-1} = 7$$

(d) $5$ in $\mathbb{Z}_{13}$

$$5 \times 8 = 40 \equiv 3 \times 13 + 1 \equiv 1 \pmod{13} \implies 5^{-1} = 8$$

Problem 1.3: Method of Squaring

Calculate each of the following powers using the method of squaring.

  1. (a) $15^{16} \pmod{17}$
  2. (b) $75^{45} \pmod{103}$
  3. (c) $13245^{74} \pmod{24179}$
View Solution

Solution & Explanation

(a) $15^{16} \pmod{17}$

Using the method of squaring:

15² ≡ 225 ≡ 4 (mod 17)
15⁴ ≡ 4² ≡ 16 ≡ -1 (mod 17)
15⁸ ≡ (-1)² ≡ 1 (mod 17)
15¹⁶ ≡ 1² ≡ 1 (mod 17)

Final Answer: $\mathbf{1}$

(b) $75^{45} \pmod{103}$ ($45 = 32 + 8 + 4 + 1$)

Calculate powers of 75 modulo 103:

75² ≡ 63 (mod 103)
75⁴ ≡ 55 (mod 103)
75⁸ ≡ 38 (mod 103)
75¹⁶ ≡ 2 (mod 103)
75³² ≡ 4 (mod 103)

Combine powers: 75⁴⁵ = 75³² x 75⁸ x 75⁴ x 75¹

75³² x 75⁸ ≡ 4 x 38 ≡ 49 (mod 103)
75⁴ x 75¹ ≡ 55 x 75 ≡ 5 (mod 103)

75⁴⁵ ≡ 49 x 5 ≡ 39 (mod 103)

Final Answer: $\mathbf{39}$

(c) $13245^{74} \pmod{24179}$ ($74 = 64 + 8 + 2$)

Calculate powers modulo 24179:

13245² ≡ 11380 (mod 24179)
13245⁴ ≡ 1676 (mod 24179)
13245⁸ ≡ 4212 (mod 24179)
13245¹⁶ ≡ 17737 (mod 24179)
13245³² ≡ 8200 (mod 24179)
13245⁶⁴ ≡ 22380 (mod 24179)

Combine the necessary powers: 13245⁷⁴ = 13245⁶⁴ x 13245⁸ x 13245²

13245⁸ x 13245² ≡ 4212 x 11380 ≡ 9782 (mod 24179)

13245⁷⁴ ≡ 13245⁶⁴ x 9782 (mod 24179)
13245⁷⁴ ≡ 22380 x 9782 ≡ 4494 (mod 24179)

Final Answer: $\mathbf{4494}$

Problem 1.4: Fermat's Little Theorem

(a) Calculate $29^{25} \pmod{11}$.

(b) Calculate $1^{10}+2^{20}+3^{30}+4^{40}+5^{50}+6^{60} \pmod{11}$.

View Solution

Solution & Explanation

Fermat's Little Theorem (FLT) states that if $p$ is a prime that does not divide $a$, then $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$. Here, $p=11$, so $a^{10} \equiv 1 \pmod{11}$ for $a \not\equiv 0 \pmod{11}$.

(a) $29^{25} \pmod{11}$

First, reduce the base: $29 = 33 - 4$, so $29 \equiv -4 \pmod{11}$.

$$\begin{aligned} 29^{25} &\equiv (-4)^{25} \pmod{11} \\ &\equiv (-4)^{10} \times (-4)^{10} \times (-4)^{5} \pmod{11} \\ &\equiv 1 \times 1 \times (-4)^5 \pmod{11} \\ &\equiv -4^5 \pmod{11} \\ &\equiv -2^{10} \pmod{11} \end{aligned}$$

By FLT, $2^{10} \equiv 1 \pmod{11}$, so:

$$29^{25} \equiv -(1) \equiv -1 \equiv 10 \pmod{11}$$

Final Answer: $\mathbf{10}$

(b) Sum of Powers Modulo 11

We use FLT: $a^{10} \equiv 1 \pmod{11}$ for $a \in \{1, 2, \dots, 6\}$.

$$\begin{aligned} &\quad 1^{10}+2^{20}+3^{30}+4^{40}+5^{50}+6^{60} \pmod{11} \\ &\equiv 1^{10} + (2^{10})^2 + (3^{10})^3 + (4^{10})^4 + (5^{10})^5 + (6^{10})^6 \pmod{11} \\ &\equiv 1 + 1^2 + 1^3 + 1^4 + 1^5 + 1^6 \pmod{11} \\ &\equiv 1 + 1 + 1 + 1 + 1 + 1 \pmod{11} \\ &\equiv 6 \pmod{11} \end{aligned}$$

Final Answer: $\mathbf{6}$

RSA Encryption

Problem 1.5: Key Generation

Use primes $p=17$ and $q=19$. Find encryption exponent $e$ and decryption exponent $d$.

View Solution

Solution & Explanation

Step 1: Compute $n$ and $z$

$$\begin{aligned} n &= p \times q = 17 \times 19 = 323 \\ z &= (p-1)(q-1) = 16 \times 18 = 288 \end{aligned}$$

Step 2: Choose Public Exponent $e$

We need $e$ such that $\gcd(e, z) = \gcd(e, 288) = 1$. The prime factorization of $288$ is $2^5 \times 3^2$. We can choose any prime not equal to 2 or 3. The solution chooses $\mathbf{e=5}$.

Step 3: Find Private Exponent $d$ (EEA)

We need $d$ such that $5d \equiv 1 \pmod{288}$, meaning $5d + 288k = 1$ for some integers $d, k$. We use the Euclidean Algorithm on (288, 5):

288 = 5(57) + 3   (1)
  5 = 3(1) + 2    (2)
  3 = 2(1) + 1    (3)
  2 = 1(2) + 0

Work backwards from (3) to express $\gcd(288, 5)=1$ as a linear combination:

1 = 3 - 2(1)               (from 3)
1 = 3 - (5 - 3)            (sub 2 from 2)
1 = 2(3) - 5
1 = 2(288 - 5(57)) - 5     (sub 3 from 1)
1 = 2(288) - 114(5) - 5
1 = 2(288) - 115(5)

We have $1 = (-115)(5) + (2)(288)$. To make the coefficient of $e=5$ positive, we use the identity $1 = (-115 + 288)(5) + (2 - 5)(288) = 173(5) - 3(288)$.

So, $5d \equiv 1 \pmod{288}$ when $d=173$.

Keys:

  • Encryption exponent $e = \mathbf{5}$
  • Decryption exponent $d = \mathbf{173}$

Problem 1.6: RSA Practice

Primes: $p=13, q=17$. Encryption exponent $e=11$.

  1. (a) Verify $e=11$ works.
  2. (b) Encrypt $m_1=52$ and $m_2=103$.
  3. (c) Verify $d=35$ works as decryption exponent.
  4. (d) Decrypt the results from (b) using $d=35$.
View Solution

Solution & Explanation

First, compute: $n = 13 \times 17 = 221$. $z = (13-1)(17-1) = 12 \times 16 = 192$.

(a) Verify $e=11$

We check $\gcd(e, z) = \gcd(11, 192)$. Since $192 = 2^6 \times 3$, and 11 is not a factor of 192, $\gcd(11, 192) = 1$. The exponent $e=11$ is valid.

(b) Encrypt 52 and 103 ($e=11, n=221$)

For $m_1=52$:

$$52^2 = 2704 = 12 \times 221 + 52 \equiv 52 \pmod{221}$$

Since $52^2 \equiv 52 \pmod{221}$, any higher power is also $52 \pmod{221}$ (i.e., $52^k \equiv 52 \pmod{221}$ for $k \ge 1$).

$$\mathbf{c_1 = 52^{11} \equiv 52 \pmod{221}}$$

For $m_2=103$:

$$103^2 = 10609 = 48 \times 221 + 1 \equiv 1 \pmod{221}$$

Since $103^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{221}$, we can use this to simplify $103^{11} = 103^{10} \times 103 = (103^2)^5 \times 103$.

$$\mathbf{c_2 = 103^{11} \equiv (1)^5 \times 103 \equiv 103 \pmod{221}}$$

(c) Verify $d=35$

We check $d \times e \equiv 1 \pmod{z}$, or $35 \times 11 \equiv 1 \pmod{192}$.

$$35 \times 11 = 385$$ $$385 = 2 \times 192 + 1 \equiv 1 \pmod{192}$$

Yes, $d=35$ works as the decryption exponent.

(d) Decrypt $c_1=52$ and $c_2=103$

For $c_1=52$, using the fact that $52^2 \equiv 52 \pmod{221}$:

$$m_1 \equiv 52^{35} \equiv 52 \pmod{221} \quad \text{(recovers 52)}$$

For $c_2=103$, using the fact that $103^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{221}$:

$$m_2 \equiv 103^{35} \equiv 103^{34} \times 103 \equiv (103^2)^{17} \times 103 \equiv 1^{17} \times 103 \equiv 103 \pmod{221} \quad \text{(recovers 103)}$$

Problem 1.7: Larger RSA Case Study

Primes $p=59, q=101$. Encryption exponent $e=41$.

  1. (a) Verify $e=41$ is valid.
  2. (b) Find decryption exponent $d$.
  3. (c) Encrypt $m=584$.
  4. (d) Decrypt $c=250$.
View Solution

Solution & Explanation

First, compute: $n = 59 \times 101 = 5959$. $z = (59-1)(101-1) = 58 \times 100 = 5800$.

(a) Verify $e=41$

We check $\gcd(e, z) = \gcd(41, 5800)$. The prime factorization of $z=5800$ is $2^3 \times 5^2 \times 29$. Since $e=41$ is a prime number and 41 does not appear in the prime factorization of 5800, $\gcd(41, 5800) = 1$. The exponent is valid.

(b) Find Decryption Exponent $d$ (EEA on 5800, 41)

We solve $41d \equiv 1 \pmod{5800}$.

Euclidean Steps:
(1)  5800 = 141(41) + 19
(2)    41 = 2(19) + 3
(3)    19 = 6(3) + 1
(4)     3 = 3(1) + 0

Backward Substitution:
1 = 19 - 6(3)
1 = 19 - 6(41 - 2(19))
1 = 13(19) - 6(41)
1 = 13(5800 - 141(41)) - 6(41)
1 = 13(5800) - 1833(41) - 6(41)
1 = 13(5800) - 1839(41)

We have $1 = (-1839)(41) + (13)(5800)$. To get a positive $d$, we use $d \equiv -1839 \pmod{5800}$:

$$d = -1839 + 5800 = 3961$$

The decryption exponent is $\mathbf{d = 3961}$.

(c) Encrypt $m=584$ (Public Key: (5959, 41))

We calculate $584^{41} \pmod{5959}$ using squaring ($41 = 32 + 8 + 1$).

584² ≡ 1393 (mod 5959)
584⁴ ≡ 1676 (mod 5959)
584⁸ ≡ 1066 (mod 5959)
584¹⁶ ≡ 4146 (mod 5959)
584³² ≡ 3560 (mod 5959)

584⁴¹ = 584³² x 584⁸ x 584¹

584⁸ x 584 ≡ 1066 x 584 ≡ 2848 (mod 5959)

584⁴¹ ≡ 3560 x 2848 ≡ 2621 (mod 5959)

The encrypted message is $\mathbf{c = 2621}$.

(d) Decrypt $c=250$ (Private Exponent: $d=3961$)

We calculate $250^{3961} \pmod{5959}$. The binary expansion of $3961$ is $2048 + 1024 + 512 + 256 + 64 + 32 + 16 + 8 + 1$.

250¹ ≡ 250 (mod 5959)
250² ≡ 2910 (mod 5959)
250⁴ ≡ 361 (mod 5959)
250⁸ ≡ 5182 (mod 5959)
250¹⁶ ≡ 1870 (mod 5959)
250³² ≡ 4926 (mod 5959)
250⁶⁴ ≡ 428 (mod 5959)
250¹²⁸ ≡ 4414 (mod 5959)
250²⁵⁶ ≡ 3398 (mod 5959)
250⁵¹² ≡ 3821 (mod 5959)
250¹⁰²⁴ ≡ 491 (mod 5959)
250²⁰⁴⁸ ≡ 2721 (mod 5959)

Intermediate products:
250²⁰⁴⁸ x 250¹⁰²⁴ ≡ 2721 x 491 ≡ 1195 (mod 5959)
250⁵¹² x 250²⁵⁶ ≡ 3821 x 3398 ≡ 5056 (mod 5959)
250⁶⁴ x 250³² ≡ 428 x 4926 ≡ 4801 (mod 5959)
250¹⁶ x 250⁸ x 250¹ ≡ 1870 x 5182 x 250 ≡ 1006 x 250 (mod 5959)

Further combination:
1195 x 5056 ≡ 5453 (mod 5959)
4801 x 1006 ≡ 3016 (mod 5959)
5453 x 3016 ≡ 5367 (mod 5959)
5367 x 250 ≡ 975 (mod 5959)

The decrypted number is $\mathbf{m = 975}$.
