Zero Divisors: Elements That Multiply to Zero

Zero divisors are special non-zero elements in modular arithmetic that cause the familiar property of cancellation to fail.

1. Formal Definition

An element $r$ in $\mathbb{Z}_m$ is called a zero divisor if it is non-zero ($r \not\equiv 0$) and there exists another non-zero element $s \in \mathbb{Z}_m$ such that their modular product is zero.

$$r \cdot s \equiv 0 \pmod{m}$$ $$\text{where } r \not\equiv 0 \text{ and } s \not\equiv 0$$

In standard integer arithmetic ($\mathbb{Z}$), this is impossible: if $r \cdot s = 0$, either $r$ or $s$ (or both) must be zero. In modular arithmetic, however, the product can be a multiple of the modulus $m$, resulting in $0 \pmod{m}$.

2. Condition for Existence

The existence of zero divisors is strictly tied to whether the modulus $m$ is composite.

Theorem: Characterization

An element $r \in \mathbb{Z}_m$ (where $r \not\equiv 0$) is a zero divisor if and only if: $$\gcd(r, m) > 1$$ If $\gcd(r, m) = 1$, the element is invertible (it has a modular inverse), not a zero divisor.

Example in $\mathbb{Z}_6$ (Composite Modulus):

  • $r=2$: $\gcd(2, 6) = 2$. $2$ is a zero divisor. We know $2 \times 3 \equiv 6 \equiv 0 \pmod{6}$.
  • $r=3$: $\gcd(3, 6) = 3$. $3$ is a zero divisor. We know $3 \times 2 \equiv 6 \equiv 0 \pmod{6}$.
  • $r=5$: $\gcd(5, 6) = 1$. $5$ is invertible ($5^{-1}=5$, since $5 \times 5 = 25 \equiv 1 \pmod{6}$).

Example in $\mathbb{Z}_7$ (Prime Modulus):

  • For every non-zero $r \in \{1, 2, \dots, 6\}$, $\gcd(r, 7) = 1$.
  • Therefore, $\mathbb{Z}_7$ has no zero divisors.

3. Consequence: The Failure of Cancellation

Zero divisors are the reason why we cannot always cancel a term from both sides of a modular congruence if the modulus is composite.

Counterexample using $\mathbb{Z}_6$:

Consider the following congruence in $\mathbb{Z}_6$:

$$3 \cdot 1 \equiv 3 \cdot 5 \pmod{6}$$ $$\text{Since } 3 \cdot 1 = 3 \text{ and } 3 \cdot 5 = 15 \equiv 3 \pmod{6}$$

If we try to cancel $3$ (which is a zero divisor), we get $1 \equiv 5 \pmod{6}$, which is false (since $5-1=4$, and $4$ is not divisible by $6$).

The Failure: The term $3(5-1) = 3(4) = 12 \equiv 0 \pmod{6}$. Because $3 \times 4 \equiv 0$, the $3$ is a zero divisor, and cancelling it is prohibited.
