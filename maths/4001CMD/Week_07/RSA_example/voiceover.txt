Jack: Great! I've heard RSA is a type of public-key cryptography. What does that actually mean?

Amanda: Excellent question. In public-key cryptography, every user has two mathematically linked keys: a public key that anyone can see, and a private key that only they know. The clever part is this — if I encrypt a message using your public key, only your private key can decrypt it. These two keys are mathematical opposites.

Jack: So it's like a padlock and a key? Anyone can lock the padlock, but only the owner can unlock it?

Amanda: That's a perfect analogy. RSA achieves this using the mathematical difficulty of factoring very large numbers. It's easy to multiply two large prime numbers together, but it's astronomically hard to reverse that — to find the two primes if you only know the product. That asymmetry is the security foundation.

Jack: Okay. So what are the actual steps involved in RSA?

Amanda: RSA has five main steps. Step one: Key Generation. We choose two large prime numbers, p and q, and compute the modulus n as p times q. We also compute the totient z, which is the product of p minus 1 and q minus 1.

Step two: Public Key Selection. We choose a public exponent e such that e is greater than 1 and less than z, and the greatest common divisor of e and z equals 1 — meaning they share no common factors other than 1.

Step three: Private Key Derivation. We calculate the private exponent d as the modular multiplicative inverse of e modulo z. This is done using the Extended Euclidean Algorithm. In plain terms, d is the number such that d times e leaves a remainder of 1 when divided by z.

Step four: Encryption. To encrypt a message m, we raise m to the power e, then take the remainder when divided by n. That gives us the ciphertext c.

Step five: Decryption. To decrypt ciphertext c, we raise c to the power d, then take the remainder when divided by n. That recovers the original message.

If an attacker doesn't know the prime factors, they can't compute z, and without z they can't find d, the private key.

When we rearrange that equation modulo 832, the k times 832 term vanishes because 832 mod 832 is zero. That leaves d times 15 congruent to 1 modulo 832, which is exactly what we need. So d is our private exponent.
