RSA Cryptography Solution

Recap: The Main Steps of RSA

0:00
  1. Key Generation: Choose two large prime numbers (\(p\) and \(q\)), compute the modulus \(n = p \times q\), and the totient \(z = (p-1)(q-1)\).
  2. Public Key (\(e\)): Select an encryption exponent \(e\) such that \(1 < e < z\) and \(\gcd(e, z) = 1\). The Public Key is \((n, e)\).
  3. Private Key (\(d\)): Calculate the decryption exponent \(d\) as the modular multiplicative inverse of \(e\) modulo \(z\). This is found using the Extended Euclidean Algorithm (EEA): \(d \cdot e \equiv 1 \pmod{z}\). The Private Key is \((n, d)\).
  4. Encryption: To encrypt a message \(m\), calculate the ciphertext \(c\) using the public key: \(c = m^e \pmod{n}\).
  5. Decryption: To decrypt the ciphertext \(c\), calculate the original message \(m'\) using the private key: \(m' = c^d \pmod{n}\).

This detailed solution applies these steps using the following specific parameters:

  • Primes: \( p = 53 \), \( q = 17 \)
  • Message: \( m = 123 \)

1 Step 1: Compute Modulus (\( n \)) and Totient (\( z \))

0:00

A. Calculate Modulus (\( n \))

\[ n = p \times q \] \[ n = 53 \times 17 = 901 \]

B. Calculate Totient (\( z \))

\[ z = (p-1)(q-1) \] \[ z = (53-1)(17-1) = 52 \times 16 = 832 \]

2 Step 2: Select the Public Exponent (\( e \))

0:00

Criteria for Choosing \( e \):

  • \( 1 < e < z \) where \( z = (p-1)(q-1) \)
  • \( \gcd(e, z) = 1 \) (coprime to \( z \))
  • Common choices: small primes like 3, 17, or 65537 for efficiency

Test Candidate Values for \( e \)

We need to find an \( e \) such that \( \gcd(e, 832) = 1 \). Let's test some candidates:

Candidate \( e \) gcd(e, 832) Valid? Reason
2 2 No 832 is even → gcd(2,832) = 2
3 1 Yes 3 is prime and doesn't divide 832
5 1 Yes 5 is prime and doesn't divide 832
7 1 Yes 7 is prime and doesn't divide 832
15 1 Yes gcd(15,832) = gcd(\(3×5, ~(2^6×13)\) = 1
17 1 Yes 17 is prime and doesn't divide 832

Select \( e = 15 \) and Verify

\[ \text{Prime factorization: } 832 = 2^6 \times 13 \] \[ \text{Prime factorization: } 15 = 3 \times 5 \] \[ \gcd(15, 832) = \gcd(3 \times 5, 2^6 \times 13) = 1 \]

The public exponent \( e = 15 \) is valid because \( \gcd(15, 832) = 1 \).

The Public Key is \( (n, e) = (901, 15) \).

3 Step 3: Find the Private Exponent (\( d \))

0:00

We find \( d \), the modular multiplicative inverse of \( e \) modulo \( z \), such that:

\[ d \times 15 \equiv 1 \pmod{832} \]

Extended Euclidean Algorithm (EEA)

We find integers \( d \) and \( k \) such that \( 15d + 832k = 1 \).

\begin{align*} 1. &\quad \text{Divide 832 by 15:} & 832 &= 55 \times 15 + 7 \\ 2. &\quad \text{Divide 15 by 7:} & 15 &= 2 \times 7 + 1 \\ 3. &\quad \text{Isolate the GCD (1):} & 1 &= 15 - 2 \times 7 \\ 4. &\quad \text{Substitute 7:} & 1 &= 15 - 2 \times (832 - 55 \times 15) \\ 5. &\quad \text{Simplify and collect:} & 1 &= 15 - 2 \times 832 + 110 \times 15 \\ & & 1 &= 111 \times 15 - 2 \times 832 \end{align*}

The private exponent is \( d = 111 \).
The Private Key is \( (n, d) = (901, 111) \).

4 Step 4: Encrypt the Message (\( m = 123 \))

0:00

We calculate the ciphertext \( c \) using the public key: \( c = m^{e} \pmod{n} \).

\[ c = 123^{15} \pmod{901} \]

Exponentiation by Squaring (Doubling Method)

The exponent is \( e = 15 \), which in binary is \( 1111_{2} \). Thus, \( 15 = 8 + 4 + 2 + 1 \).

\begin{align*} c &\equiv 579 \times 205 \times 713 \times 123 \pmod{901} \\ 1. &\quad 579 \times 205 = 118695 \equiv 664 \pmod{901} \\ 2. &\quad 713 \times 123 = 87699 \equiv 302 \pmod{901} \\ 3. &\quad c \equiv 664 \times 302 \pmod{901} \\ 4. &\quad c \equiv 200528 \pmod{901} \\ 5. &\quad 200528 = 222 \times 901 + 506 \\ &\quad c = 506 \end{align*}

The Encrypted Ciphertext is \( c = 506 \).

5 Step 5: Decrypt the Ciphertext (\( c = 506 \))

0:00

We use the private key \( d \) to recover the original message \( m' \): \( m' = c^{d} \pmod{n} \).

\[ m' = 506^{111} \pmod{901} \]

Note on Public Exponent \( e \) Selection

In practice, RSA implementations often use fixed public exponents like 3, 17, or 65537 because they have a small number of 1-bits in their binary representation, which makes modular exponentiation more efficient. The value 65537 (5th Fermat prime, \( F_n=2^{2n}+1, ~ n=0,1,2,3,4\) ) is particularly common in modern implementations as it provides a good balance between security and performance.
