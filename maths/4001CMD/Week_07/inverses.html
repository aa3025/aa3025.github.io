The Modular Inverse: The Key to Division

A modular inverse is the equivalent of division in modular arithmetic, essential for operations like RSA decryption.

1. Formal Definition

For an integer $a$ and a modulus $m$, the modular inverse of $a$ (denoted $a^{-1}$) is an integer $x$ such that when $a$ is multiplied by $x$, the result is congruent to $1$ modulo $m$.

$$a \cdot x \equiv 1 \pmod{m}$$ $$\text{where } x = a^{-1}$$

The inverse $x$ allows us to effectively "divide by $a$" in $\mathbb{Z}_m$. It is not a fraction; it is another integer in the set $\mathbb{Z}_m$.

2. When Does an Inverse Exist?

A modular inverse $a^{-1}$ modulo $m$ exists if and only if $a$ and $m$ are relatively prime.

The necessary and sufficient condition for $a^{-1}$ to exist modulo $m$ is: $$\gcd(a, m) = 1$$ If $\gcd(a, m) > 1$, then $a$ is a zero divisor and has no inverse.

Example in $\mathbb{Z}_7$:

  • Find $3^{-1} \pmod{7}$: Since $\gcd(3, 7) = 1$, the inverse exists. We find $3 \times 5 = 15 \equiv 1 \pmod{7}$. Thus, $3^{-1} = 5$.

Example in $\mathbb{Z}_6$:

  • Find $2^{-1} \pmod{6}$: Since $\gcd(2, 6) = 2 \neq 1$, the inverse does not exist. $2$ is a zero divisor ($2 \times 3 \equiv 0 \pmod{6}$).

3. Finding the Inverse using the Extended Euclidean Algorithm (EEA)

When $\gcd(a, m) = 1$, the EEA finds integers $x$ and $y$ such that:

$$a \cdot x + m \cdot y = 1$$

If we take this equation and reduce it modulo $m$, the $m \cdot y$ term drops out because $m \cdot y \equiv 0 \pmod{m}$:

$$a \cdot x + m \cdot y \equiv 1 \pmod{m}$$ $$a \cdot x + 0 \equiv 1 \pmod{m}$$ $$\mathbf{a \cdot x \equiv 1 \pmod{m}}$$

This means the integer $x$ found by the Extended Euclidean Algorithm is the modular inverse of $a$ (i.e., $x = a^{-1}$). This is the precise technique used to find the RSA private key $d$, which is the inverse of $e$ modulo $z = \phi(n)$.
