Slide 12: The Contrapositive
Amanda: The contrapositive of "p implies q" is the statement "not q implies not p". You essentially negate both sides AND flip the arrow.

Amanda: Perfect. And here is the magic trick of logic: Unlike the converse, the contrapositive is absolutely always logically equivalent to the original implication! They say the exact same thing chemically!
Jack: Why is that difference useful?
Amanda: Contrapositives are an absolute lifesaver. Often, in a proof, you'll be blocked because trying to push P to reach Q algebraically results in a chaotic, messy system of symbols. Slide 15: Contradiction
Amanda: Another wildly famous proof tactic is Proof by Contradiction. A contradiction occurs when our formal derivations lead us to a statement 'r' being true, but its negation, 'not r', is also true. 
Jack: But that's physically impossible. You can't have a mathematical statement be true and false at the exact same moment.

Slide 16: Contradiction (Formal)

Jack: Wow, and then line four immediately says: "therefore, not p, or q". We just applied De Morgan's Law to line three! By distributing the 'not', our 'and' neatly flipped right into an 'or'. 
Amanda: Excellent. Finally, we draw one last horizontal deduction line. The final line reads: "therefore, p implies q". Slide 17: Rules of Inference

Slide 18: Modus tollens
Amanda: Let's extract a universal shortcut from that exact proof structure. Look at the first column on the slide; it's the exact deduction we just used. Line one: "quantity p and not q, implies quantity r and not r". 
Jack: And line two is the strict negation: "not, quantity r and not r". Then we have a horizontal line, and line three reads: "therefore, not, quantity p and not q".
Amanda: Right. Now, to make this a universally reusable shortcut, we abstract those messy statements into simpler overall variables. Let's declare capital P to mean "quantity p and not q", and capital Q to mean "quantity r and not r". Look at the second block.
Jack: Ah! The second block mathematically substitutes them. Line one neatly becomes: "capital P implies capital Q". Line two becomes "not capital Q". And the horizontal deduction line simplifies down to: "therefore, not capital P"!
Amanda: Perfect pronunciation! This generalized rule of inference is known as Modus Tollens, Latin for 'the way that denies by denying'. And notice the equation at the very bottom?
Jack: Oh wow, "capital P implies capital Q" is logically equivalent to "not capital Q implies not capital P". That's exactly our old friend the Contrapositive!
Amanda: Fantastic connection! Modus Tollens relies on the absolute algebraic backbone of the contrapositive. If capital Q did NOT happen, then absolutely certain, capital P could not have happened. This forms the core logic for diagnosing software bugs everywhere. 
Jack: Like saying 'If the database works, the site loads.' But if the site didn't load? Then the database definitely broke.
Amanda: Yes. Modus Tollens in everyday engineering.

Slide 19: Modus ponens
Amanda: The sister rule to Modus Tollens is Modus Ponens. Translating to the 'way that affirms'. If 'p implies q', and we are given that 'p' actually happens... Therefore?
Jack: P went in, so Q must inevitably come out. Therefore Q is true! 
Amanda: This one is the bread and butter of all code execution. You see this everywhere in standard programming execution flow. While Modus Tollens was the 'way that denies', Modus Ponens is straightforward affirmation. If it rains, the ground gets wet. It is raining. Ergo, the ground is wet. If an IF statement in code evaluates to true, run the inner block. Basic, sequential affirmation.

Slide 20: Ponens vs. Tollens
Amanda: Because they handle opposite sides of the implication, it is very common for students to mistake these for their fallacious counterparts. 
Jack: I really have to mentally double check this so I don't use a fallacy accidentally. Ponens is 'P happened, so Q happens'. Tollens is 'Q didn't happen, so P couldn't have happened'. 
Amanda: Remember the golden rule: You cannot successfully affirm the conclusion directly, and you cannot legitimately deny the hypothesis directly. Affirming the hypothesis, which is ponens, and Denying the conclusion, which is tollens, are the ONLY valid legal moves on the board!

Slide 21: Other rules of inference
Amanda: There are many more mini-tools in our logical toolkit. Take Hypothetical Syllogism for example. If 'p implies q', and 'q implies r', then what?
Jack: It's like a chain reaction! P implies r! P knocks down Q, and Q immediately knocks down R. It sets up dominoes that perfectly knock one another down dynamically.
Amanda: Exactly! We also have the Addition rule. If 'p' is proven true, we can safely jump to say the block of 'p or q' is entirely true, regardless of what mysterious value q actually holds! Each of these tools allows us to gracefully prune or expand our mathematical formulas on the fly.

Slide 22: De Morgan's Laws
Amanda: Now we reach one of the most critical laws in computer science: De Morgan's Laws. Let's look at the very first block on the slide, pulling the second inference from our earlier contradiction proof. Line one: "not, quantity p and not q". This logically inferred line two: "therefore, not p, or q".
Jack: And just like we did with Modus Tollens, we can wrap those small statements into clean variables! The slide says let's call capital P the statement 'p', and capital Q the statement 'not q'. 
Amanda: Exactly! Applying that substitution gives the second block. Our premise becomes: "not, quantity capital P and capital Q". And our inferred conclusion becomes: "therefore, not capital P... or, not capital Q". 
Jack: Wait, the original conclusion had a regular 'q' on the right side. But since we dynamically defined capital Q as 'not q', then 'not capital Q' means 'not, not q'... which simplifies right back to regular 'q'! That mathematical mapping holds perfectly!
Amanda: You nailed it! That structure is the first of De Morgan's laws. And the other equally famous law is right below it. "Not, quantity capital P or capital Q"... infers: "therefore, not capital P, and, not capital Q".
Jack: These rules are so robust they're written out as absolute equivalencies in the bottom box. "not, quantity capital P and capital Q, is logically equivalent to, not capital P, or, not capital Q". And vice versa where the 'and' flips to an 'or'.
Amanda: Yes! A great way to verbally remember De Morgan's Law is 'Distribute the NOT, and flip the sign'. Just like pushing a negative multiplier into parentheses in basic algebra, you literally multiply the NOT into the variables individually, and immediately flip your ANDs into ORs, and your ORs into ANDs. 
Jack: I can absolutely see how that is incredibly practical for unwinding tricky, massive nested negations when writing SQL queries or conditional logic.

Slide 23: Algebraic properties
Amanda: By substituting equivalent statements, applying De Morgan's laws, and using spatial properties like Commutativity and Associativity, we effectively perform boolean algebra!
